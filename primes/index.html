A Prime-Generating Constant

In 2017 my friend Juli asked himself this question: Is it possible to generate the prime numbers by starting with a constant (\(f_1=2. \cdots \)) that when rounded down gives the first prime (\( \lfloor f_1 \rfloor\ = p_1 = 2 \)), and then by multiplying this prime by some other real number encoded in \(f_1\), we can reach a value \(f_2=3. \cdots \) that rounds to the next prime (3), and so on. Expressed mathmematically:

\[ \large f_{n+1} = \lfloor f_n \rfloor \cdot G(f_n) ~~~~~~~~~~ \lfloor f_n \rfloor = p_n \]

What do we know about this multiplicative factor \( G(f_n) \)? We can infer that

\[ G(f_n) = \frac{f_{n+1}}{\lfloor f_n \rfloor} = \frac{f_{n+1}}{p_n} \lt \frac{p_n }{p_n} \]

\[ \large \lambda = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{p_k -1}{\prod_{i=1}^{k-1} p_i} = \frac{2 - 1}{1} + \frac{3 - 1}{2} + \frac{5 - 1}{2 \cdot 3} + \frac{7 - 1}{2 \cdot 3 \cdot 5} + \dots \]
